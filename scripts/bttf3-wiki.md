_**Back to the Future Part III**_ is a 1990 American [science fiction Western film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction_Western "Science fiction Western") and the third installment of the [_Back to the Future_ trilogy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_(franchise) "Back to the Future (franchise)"). The film was directed by [Robert Zemeckis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Zemeckis "Robert Zemeckis"), and stars [Michael J. Fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_J._Fox "Michael J. Fox"), [Christopher Lloyd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Lloyd "Christopher Lloyd"), [Mary Steenburgen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Steenburgen "Mary Steenburgen"), [Thomas F. Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_F._Wilson "Thomas F. Wilson"), and [Lea Thompson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lea_Thompson "Lea Thompson"). The film continues immediately after _[Back to the Future Part II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_II "Back to the Future Part II")_ (1989); while stranded in 1955 during his time travel adventures, [Marty McFly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marty_McFly "Marty McFly") (Fox) discovers that his friend [Dr. Emmett "Doc" Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmett_Brown "Emmett Brown") (Lloyd) is trapped in 1885 and was killed by [Biff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biff_Tannen "Biff Tannen")'s great-grandfather [Buford "Mad Dog" Tannen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buford_%22Mad_Dog%22_Tannen "Buford \"Mad Dog\" Tannen") (Wilson). Marty travels to 1885 to rescue Doc and return to 1985, but matters are complicated when Doc falls in love with [Clara Clayton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Back_to_the_Future_characters "List of Back to the Future characters") (Steenburgen).

_Back to the Future Part III_ was filmed in [California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California "California") and [Arizona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arizona "Arizona"), and was produced on a $40 million budget [back-to-back](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back-to-back_film_production "Back-to-back film production") with _[Part II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_II "Back to the Future Part II")_. _Part III_ was released in the United States on May 25, 1990, six months after the previous installment, and grossed $245 million worldwide during its initial run, making it the [sixth-highest-grossing film of 1990](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1990_in_film#Highest-grossing_films "1990 in film").[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-4) The film received a positive response from critics, who noted it as an improvement over _Part II_.

## Plot

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Back_to_the_Future_Part_III&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Plot")\]

In November 1955, moments after witnessing [Emmett "Doc" Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmett_Brown "Emmett Brown") disappear in his [DeLorean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DeLorean_time_machine "DeLorean time machine"), [Marty McFly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marty_McFly "Marty McFly") learns that Doc was sent 70 years in the past to 1885.[\[N 1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-5) Using information from Doc's 1885 letter, Marty and the 1955 Doc find and repair the DeLorean so Marty can return to 1985. However, after finding it, Marty comes across a tombstone with Doc's name, with the inscription stating that Doc was shot by [Biff Tannen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biff_Tannen "Biff Tannen")'s great-grandfather, Buford "Mad Dog" Tannen, six days after writing the letter.

Despite the letter's warning, Marty travels back to September 2, 1885, to save Doc, arriving in the middle of a [cavalry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Cavalry "United States Cavalry") pursuit of [Native Americans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_California "Indigenous peoples of California"), but the car's fuel line is damaged in the process. Chased by a [bear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_black_bear "American black bear"), he is knocked out and found by his [Irish-born](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irish_nationality_law "Irish nationality law") great-great-grandparents Seamus and Maggie McFly, who allow him to stay for the night. The next morning, under the alias [Clint Eastwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clint_Eastwood "Clint Eastwood"), Marty arrives in a newly founded [Hill Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_Valley_(Back_to_the_Future) "Hill Valley (Back to the Future)") but runs afoul of Buford and his gang. Buford tries hanging Marty but fails when Doc rescues him. Doc agrees to leave 1885 after learning his fate, but without gasoline, the DeLorean cannot reach its required 88 miles per hour (142 kilometers per hour). He thus proposes using a [steam locomotive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_locomotive "Steam locomotive") to push the DeLorean to that speed.

While inspecting a [rail spur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branch_line "Branch line"), Doc saves the town's new schoolteacher Clara Clayton from falling into the ravine, averting her death from the original timeline. They fall in love at first sight and form a close relationship. At a town festival for the courthouse, Buford tries shooting Doc, but Marty thwarts him. Buford then challenges him to a showdown in two days by calling him " [yellow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cowardice "Cowardice")"; an angry Marty accepts, believing that he and Doc would have left by then. Doc urges Marty not to react to provocation, letting slip that Marty has a life changing accident in the future.

Although he is reluctant to return to 1985, Doc eventually visits Clara to end their relationship and bid her goodbye. However, feeling insulted, she dismisses his story about being from the future. Despondent, he goes for a [binge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binge_drinking "Binge drinking"). In the morning, Buford arrives for Marty, who sees his alias appear in the photograph of the tombstone and refuses to duel. Doc passes out after downing just one shot; he is eventually revived, but is captured by Buford's gang, forcing Marty into the duel. Marty fools Buford into believing that he was fatally shot and knocks him into a wagon of [manure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manure "Manure"). Buford and his gang are promptly arrested for an earlier robbery.

On the train to [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco "San Francisco"), Clara learns how heartbroken Doc is and runs back to town. She finds the model of the time machine at his shop. Realizing that Doc was telling the truth, she heads back to meet him. Using the stolen locomotive, Doc and Marty push the DeLorean along the spur line. Clara boards the locomotive and, attempting to reach Doc, nearly falls off. Marty, in the DeLorean, passes his [hoverboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hoverboard "Hoverboard") to Doc, and he uses it to save Clara and carry her to safety. Marty hits 88 mph and vanishes as the overheated locomotive falls off the unfinished bridge.

Arriving back in 1985 on October 27, Marty escapes from the powerless DeLorean just before an oncoming freight train destroys it. Reuniting with his girlfriend [Jennifer Parker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Back_to_the_Future_characters#Jennifer_Parker "List of Back to the Future characters"), Marty declines a street race with Douglas J. Needles, thus avoiding the future accident Doc warned him about. Jennifer opens the fax message she kept from 2015 and watches as the text regarding Marty's firing disappears.

As Marty and Jennifer examine the DeLorean wreckage, a steam locomotive suddenly appears, operated by Doc, Clara, and their sons. Doc gives Marty a photo of them standing next to the town clock in 1885. When Jennifer asks Doc about the blank fax, he states that it means that their future has not yet been written and encourages them to make it a good one. Doc and his family bid farewell and fly off in the locomotive to an unknown time.

## Production

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Back_to_the_Future_Part_III&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Production")\]

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/DeLoreanBack2FutureIIIMontereyAug2011.JPG/250px-DeLoreanBack2FutureIIIMontereyAug2011.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DeLoreanBack2FutureIIIMontereyAug2011.JPG) One of the DeLorean vehicles used in the film[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Universal_Orlando_-_Universal_Studios_-_Back_To_The_Future_Train_Back_to_the_Future.jpg/250px-Universal_Orlando_-_Universal_Studios_-_Back_To_The_Future_Train_Back_to_the_Future.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Universal_Orlando_-_Universal_Studios_-_Back_To_The_Future_Train_Back_to_the_Future.jpg) The Time Train used at the end of the film, currently on display at [Universal Studios Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_Studios_Florida "Universal Studios Florida")

The origins of the [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_(genre) "Western (genre)") theme for _Back to the Future Part III_ lay in the production of the original _[Back to the Future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future "Back to the Future")_ film. During filming for the original, director [Robert Zemeckis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Zemeckis "Robert Zemeckis") asked actor [Michael J. Fox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_J._Fox "Michael J. Fox") what time period he would like to see. Fox replied that he wanted to visit the [Old West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_frontier "American frontier") and meet [cowboys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cowboy "Cowboy"). Zemeckis and writer/producer [Bob Gale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Gale "Bob Gale") were intrigued by the idea, but held it off until _Part III_.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint1-7) Rather than use existing sets, the filmmakers built the 1885 Hill Valley from scratch.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint1-7) The western scenes were filmed on location in [Oak Park, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oak_Park,_California "Oak Park, California") and [Monument Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monument_Valley "Monument Valley").[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-DVD(C)-8) Some of the location shooting for the 1885 Hill Valley was done in [Jamestown, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamestown,_California "Jamestown, California") and on a purpose-built set at the Red Hills Ranch near [Sonora, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonora,_California "Sonora, California").[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-DVD(C)-8) Some of the train scenes were filmed at [Railtown 1897 State Historic Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Railtown_1897_State_Historic_Park "Railtown 1897 State Historic Park"),[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-9)[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-10) a [heritage line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heritage_railroad "Heritage railroad") in Jamestown.

The shooting of the _[Back to the Future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_(franchise) "Back to the Future (franchise)")_ sequels, which were shot [back-to-back](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_back_film_production "Back to back film production") throughout 1989, reunited much of the crew of the original.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11) The films were shot over the course of eleven months, save for a three-week hiatus between filming of _Parts II_ and _III_, and concluded in January 1990. The most grueling part was editing _Part II_ while filming _Part III_, and Zemeckis bore the brunt of the process over a three-week period. While Zemeckis was shooting most of the train sequences in Sonora, Gale was in Los Angeles supervising the final dub of _Part II_.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11) Zemeckis would wrap photography and board a private plane to [Burbank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burbank,_California "Burbank, California"), where Gale and engineers would greet him on the dubbing stage with dinner. He would oversee the reels completed that day, and make changes where needed.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11) Afterwards, he would retire to the [Sheraton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheraton_Hotels_and_Resorts "Sheraton Hotels and Resorts") Universal Hotel for the night. The following morning, Zemeckis would drive to the [Burbank Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burbank_Airport "Burbank Airport"), board a flight back to the set in Northern California, and continue to shoot the film.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11)

Although the schedule for most of the personnel involved was grueling, the actors found the remote location for _Part III_ relaxing, compared to shooting its predecessor.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11)

The role of Clara Clayton was written with [Mary Steenburgen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Steenburgen "Mary Steenburgen") in mind. When she received the script, however, she was reluctant to commit to the film until her children, who had loved the first film, "hounded" her.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11)

The Hill Valley Festival Dance scene proved to be the most dangerous for Lloyd and Steenburgen; overzealous dancing left Steenburgen with a torn ligament in her foot.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint1-7)

The film also featured [cameo appearances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameo_appearance "Cameo appearance") from veteran Western film actors [Pat Buttram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pat_Buttram "Pat Buttram"), [Harry Carey Jr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Carey_Jr. "Harry Carey Jr.") and [Dub Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dub_Taylor "Dub Taylor") as three "saloon old timers".[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-12) The inclusion of these noticeable Western actors was promoted in several documentaries about the film, as well as the behind-the-scenes documentary of the DVD and in the obituary of one of the actors.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-13) Zemeckis considered having [Ronald Reagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan "Ronald Reagan") play the part of the Mayor of Hill Valley in 1885. Reagan, who in 1989 had just recently finished his second term as president, had appeared in several Westerns during his earlier career as an actor. Zemeckis contacted Reagan's agent [Lew Wasserman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lew_Wasserman "Lew Wasserman"), to gauge the ex-president's interest in the role, to which Reagan declined.[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-14)

Shooting a film set in the Old West was appealing to the [stuntmen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stunt_performer "Stunt performer"), who were all experienced horse riders. Gale recalled in 2002 that many stuntmen in Hollywood wanted to work on _Part III_.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11) Thomas F. Wilson, who played Buford Tannen, chose to perform his own stunts and spent a great deal of time learning to ride a horse and throw his [lariat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lasso "Lasso"). Filming was paused when Fox's father died, and when his son was born.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint1-7)

[Alan Silvestri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Silvestri "Alan Silvestri") returned to compose the [score](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Film_score "Film score") for _Back to the Future Part III_, continuing his longtime collaboration with Zemeckis. Rather than dictate how the music should sound, Zemeckis directed Silvestri as he would an actor, seeking to evoke emotion and treating every piece of music like a character.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11) The musicians of the Old West-style band in the film were played by American rock band [ZZ Top](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZZ_Top "ZZ Top").

The photography in _Part III_ was a "dream" for cinematographer [Dean Cundey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dean_Cundey "Dean Cundey"), who shared with much of the crew his excitement to shoot a Western. Zemeckis wished to create a spectacular climax to the film. He coordinated the actors, a live 4-6-0 ten wheeler [steam locomotive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_locomotive "Steam locomotive") (the [Sierra Railway 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierra_Railway_3 "Sierra Railway 3")[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-The_Providence_Journal_2012-15)[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-Sierra_DinnerTrain_2014-16)), [pyrotechnics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrotechnics "Pyrotechnics"), and special effects, and countless technicians all at once.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-dvdint2-11) As they had done with the previous two films in the trilogy, the visual effects for _Part III_ were managed by effects company [Industrial Light & Magic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Light_%26_Magic "Industrial Light & Magic"); the head of its animation department, [Wes Takahashi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wes_Takahashi "Wes Takahashi"), returned to once again animate the DeLorean's time travel sequences.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-17)[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-18)

On November 8, 1990, [MCA/Universal Home Video](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_Pictures_Home_Entertainment "Universal Pictures Home Entertainment") released _Back to the Future Part III_ on VHS and on December 17, 2002, on DVD.[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-19) It debuted on [Blu-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blu-ray "Blu-ray") in 2010 for the film's 20th anniversary, followed by a second [Blu-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blu-ray "Blu-ray") remaster in 2015 for the film's 25th anniversary and a [4K Blu-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra_HD_Blu-ray "Ultra HD Blu-ray") remaster in 2020 for the film's 30th anniversary.[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-20)[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-21)[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-22)

The soundtrack was released under [Varèse Sarabande](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Var%C3%A8se_Sarabande "Varèse Sarabande") on May 29, 1990, and features most of the score by Alan Silvestri and the orchestral version of the song " [Doubleback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubleback_(song) "Doubleback (song)")" performed at the festival in 1885 during the film.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-23) A two-disc special edition was released on October 12, 2015, in commemoration of the film's 25th anniversary, which includes the original score (26 tracks) on disc one and an arrangement of alternate cues and source music on a second disc.[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-24)

## Reception

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Back_to_the_Future_Part_III&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Reception")\]

### Box office

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Back_to_the_Future_Part_III&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Box office")\]

The film grossed $23 million in its first weekend of U.S. release and $87.6 million altogether in U.S. box office receipts (or about $180.7 million adjusted as of 2023[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-inflation-USGDP-25)) – $246 million worldwide.[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-26)[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-27)[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-28)

### Critical response

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Back_to_the_Future_Part_III&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Critical response")\]

On the [review aggregator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Review_aggregator "Review aggregator") website [Rotten Tomatoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes "Rotten Tomatoes"), 79% of 48 critics' reviews are positive, with an average rating of 6.8/10. The website's consensus reads: " _Back to the Future Part III_ draws the trilogy to a satisfying close with a simpler, sweeter round of time-travel antics."[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-Rotten_Tomatoes-29) [Metacritic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metacritic "Metacritic"), which uses a [weighted average](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weighted_arithmetic_mean "Weighted arithmetic mean"), assigned the film a score of 55 out of 100, based on 19 critics, indicating "mixed or average" reviews.[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-Metacritic-30) Audiences polled by [CinemaScore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CinemaScore "CinemaScore") gave the film an average grade of "A−" on an A+ to F scale, same as the second installment.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-31)

[Kim Newman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Newman "Kim Newman") of _[Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_(magazine) "Empire (magazine)")_ gave the film four out of five stars, saying that the film "restores heart interest of the first film and has a satisfying complete storyline". He praised Michael J. Fox for "keeping the plot on the move" and mentioned that Christopher Lloyd and Mary Steenburgen's romance was "funny". He said that the film's ending was the "neatest of all" and it "features one of the best time machines in the cinema, promising that this is indeed the very last in the series and neatly wrapping it up for everybody".[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-32)

[Leonard Maltin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Maltin "Leonard Maltin") preferred the film to the first two, giving it three-and-a-half stars out of four, praising the film as great fun, special effects and imagination, also saying that the movie magic works in the film.[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-33) Michael McWhertor of the website _[Polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon_(website) "Polygon (website)")_ wrote that while the film was not better than the original entry in the series, it is nonetheless "leagues better than the second"; he praised the film's comedic and romantic elements, and commended Thomas F. Wilson's performance as "Mad Dog" Tannen.[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-34)

[Roger Ebert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Ebert "Roger Ebert") of the _[Chicago Sun-Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times "Chicago Sun-Times")_ gave the film two-and-a-half out of four stars. He said that the film's western motifs are "a sitcom version that looks exactly as if it were built on a [back lot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_lot_(film) "Back lot (film)") somewhere".[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-35) Although [Vincent Canby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincent_Canby "Vincent Canby") of _[The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times")_ praised Christopher Lloyd's performance in the film, he also said that the film "looks as if it could be the beginning of a continuing television series". He complained that the film is "so sweet-natured and bland that it is almost instantly forgettable".[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-36)

Commentators noticed parallels between _Part III_ and the film _[Time After Time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_After_Time_(1979_film) "Time After Time (1979 film)")_ (1979).[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-mix979fm-37) Mary Steenburgen has said:

> Actually, I've played the same scene in that film \[ _Time After Time_\] and in \[ _Back to the Future Part III_\]…. I've had a man from a different time period tell me that he's in love with me, but he has to go back to his own time. My response in both cases is, of course, disbelief, and I order them out of my life. Afterwards, I find out I was wrong and that, in fact, the man is indeed from another time, and I go after him (them) to profess my love. It's a pretty strange feeling to find yourself doing the same scene, so many years apart, for the second time in your career.[\[37\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-steenburgen-38)

The casting of Steenburgen for _Back to the Future Part III_ appears to be deliberately intended to mirror the earlier role.[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-rejects-39)[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-thefilmbox-40) In _Time After Time_, the woman lives in the 20th century and the time traveler is from the 19th. In _Back to the Future Part III_, the woman inhabits the 19th century and the time traveler is from the 20th.[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-thefilmbox-40)

### Accolades

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Back_to_the_Future_Part_III&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Accolades")\]

In 1990, the film won a [Saturn Award for Best Music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Music "Saturn Award for Best Music") for [Alan Silvestri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Silvestri "Alan Silvestri") and a [Best Supporting Actor award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actor_(Film) "Saturn Award for Best Supporting Actor (Film)") for Thomas F. Wilson.[\[40\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-41) In 2003, it received an AOL Movies DVD Premiere Award for Best Special Edition of the Year, an award based on consumer online voting.[\[41\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-42)

## Future

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Back_to_the_Future_Part_III&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Future")\]

Co-writer and director Robert Zemeckis and co-writer Bob Gale, who have an agreement with Spielberg and Amblin that any further instalments in the _Back to the Future_ franchise will not be made without their involvement, have stated that another film is "not going to happen".[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-43) Gale commented that he did not wish to see another film in the series without the Marty McFly character nor any other actor than Michael J. Fox playing him, while acknowledging that Fox's [current health condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_J._Fox#Parkinson's_disease "Michael J. Fox") would make this impossible. He illustrated this at a 2008 fan convention in Florida, stating: "The idea of making another _Back to the Future_ movie without Michael J. Fox – you know, that's like saying, 'I'm going to cook you a steak dinner and I'm going to hold the beef.'"[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-44) Gale also said that the [Telltale video-game adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future:_The_Game "Back to the Future: The Game") is the closest thing to what a fourth film could be like.[\[44\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-45) In an interview on October 21, 2015, the day of [Marty McFly's purported arrival in the future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_II#Depiction_of_the_future "Back to the Future Part II"), [Christopher Lloyd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Lloyd "Christopher Lloyd") stated that he would consider making a fourth film under the condition that the original cast and creative team returned, along with a story "worth telling".[\[45\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-46) The same day, Lloyd reprised his role as [Doc Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmett_Brown "Emmett Brown") in a brief segment in which the character returns with a special message marking the 2015 date.[\[46\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-47)[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-48) In 2020, actor [Tom Holland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Holland "Tom Holland") claimed in an interview with [BBC Radio 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_Radio_1 "BBC Radio 1") that he was approached by an unnamed producer over a possible reboot of the franchise with him starring the lead role as Marty McFly (or a similarly new character). However, Holland stated that he was reluctant to take up this offer as he described the existing films as "perfect films", though he would be interested in re-creating scenes from the films in a deep-fake homage video or short film.[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back_to_the_Future_Part_III#cite_note-49)
